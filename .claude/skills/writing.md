# Writing Skill

## Purpose

These rules govern all prose intended for human readers: documents, reports,
articles, essays, emails, summaries, or anything longer than a few sentences.
File types include .docx, .md, .txt, and .html, but also proactively use for
generating prose.

# 1. Voice and Style

## 1.1 Tone

Confident and declarative. State things. When uncertain, say why rather than
piling on qualifiers. No sardonic tone or dry wit.

No sycophantic openings (“Great question!”, “Absolutely!”). No unnecessary
hedging stacks (“may potentially often typically”). No over‑explanation of
obvious context. Take positions rather than presenting balanced mush. Trust the
reader’s intelligence.

## 1.2 Sentences

Short by default. Longer ones earned through necessity, not habit. Active voice
almost always: the subject does the thing. Passive voice only when the actor is
genuinely unknown or unimportant.

## 1.3 Word Choice

Short words over long. “Use” not “utilize,” “but” not “however,” “buy” not
“purchase.” No jargon without explanation. No decorative adjectives.

## 1.4 Flow

Ideas connect through logic, not connector words. The next sentence follows from
the previous one because the argument demands it, not because “moreover” is
stapled on.

## 1.5 Paragraphs

Keep paragraph lengths roughly consistent within a piece. Avoid alternating
between one‑sentence paragraphs and eight‑sentence blocks.

## 1.6 Content Quality

Use specific examples, real names, actual numbers. Avoid vague authority claims
(“Research shows…”, “Experts agree…”). Have a point of view.

# 2. Typography and Mechanics

## 2.1 Quotation Marks

Use smart (curly) quotation marks in prose: “ and ” for doubles, ‘ and ’ for
singles. Never use straight quotes in finished text. Quotations must be italic.

## 2.2 Dashes and Hyphens

No em‑dashes (—). Replace with commas, parentheses, colons, or rewrite. This
rule is absolute.

Use non‑breaking hyphens (‑) for compounds (e.g. well‑known, cost‑effective) so
they do not split across lines.

## 2.3 Semicolons

Rare. Used only when two closely related independent clauses genuinely belong in
one sentence. Never as a substitute for a period.

## 2.4 Abbreviations

If an abbreviation is not immediately obvious to the audience, introduce the
full term first with the abbreviation in parentheses at first occurrence. Use
the abbreviation consistently from that point on. Common abbreviations that need
no introduction (e.g. US, UK, GDP, CEO) can be used directly.

## 2.5 Formatting

Bullet points only for genuinely discrete items, never for prose. Headers only
when document length requires navigation. No bold emphasis within prose. Default
to minimal formatting.

# 3. Blocklists

## 3.1 Banned Words

delve, tapestry, crucial, nuanced, multifaceted, realm, landscape
(metaphorical), foster, leverage, navigate (metaphorical), embark, underscore,
intricate, meticulous, robust, pivotal, paramount, comprehensive, testament,
kaleidoscope, bustling, bespoke, hitherto, cognizant, indelible, enigmatic,
noteworthy

## 3.2 Banned Connectors

furthermore, moreover, additionally, notably, consequently, subsequently,
indeed, arguably, essentially, ultimately

## 3.3 Banned Verbs

elevate, captivate, unpack, harness, unleash, streamline, facilitate,
illuminate, transcend, spearhead, propel, unlock, unveil

## 3.4 Banned Phrases

Openings: “In today’s fast‑paced world,” “In the ever‑evolving landscape of…,”
“As we navigate the complexities of…,” “In the realm of…”

Closings: “In conclusion…,” “In summary…,” “In essence…,” “Overall…,” any
restatement of what was just covered.

Clichés: “Represents a paradigm shift,” “Speaks to the broader implications,”
“Sheds light on,” “Paves the way for,” “A testament to,” “At its core,” “It’s
worth noting that,” “Game‑changer.”

## 3.5 Banned Structures

- “It’s not X, it’s Y” or “This isn’t just X, it’s Y”
- Automatic tricolons (grouping in threes)
- Five‑paragraph essay format unless explicitly requested
- Uniform sentence lengths throughout

# 4. Review Checklist

Before finalizing any document, scan for: banned vocabulary, em‑dashes, straight
quotes, non‑italic quotations, forbidden structures, and clichéd phrases.
Rewrite any violations.

# Core Principle

Argue clearly, write tightly. Short words, short sentences, active voice. Let
logic connect ideas, not filler words. Plain language over formal inflation.
Directness over comprehensiveness.
